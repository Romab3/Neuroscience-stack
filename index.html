The Stack

A four-layer coaching framework, drawing on the neuroscience of behaviour change.

Most change efforts fail not because the person lacks discipline, but because they begin at the wrong layer. The brain has a sequence it needs to move through, and skipping a layer is why insight rarely turns into action.

This tool walks through four layers in order. Each one builds on the layer beneath it. Move through it slowly, and use your own life as the case study.

Before you begin

What follows is a coaching framework, not a neuroscience course. The four layers draw on ideas from cognitive and affective neuroscience, including predictive processing, threat and safety appraisal, habit research, and dopamine and reward systems, but they are presented here as a working structure for thinking about change, not as a settled description of the brain.

The directions are well-supported by research, the specific mechanisms named in each layer are simplifications. Treat this as a lens for your own work and your coaching conversations, not as a textbook.

The precondition
01

Regulate the State

A dysregulated nervous system cannot learn, cannot form new habits, and cannot perceive reward accurately. This is why someone in chronic stress will tell you they know what they should do, and still not do it, and why telling them to try harder makes it worse.

Regulation is not the first step in change, it is the precondition for any of the other layers working at all.

In real life
  • The team member who keeps missing deadlines may be in chronic activation, not in poor performance.
  • The client who cannot make a decision may have a nervous system that reads decision-making as threat.
  • The version of you that scrolls instead of starting the task is often regulating, badly but effectively.

The environment
02

Create Safety

The nervous system is constantly asking whether this situation, this relationship, this conversation, this workplace is safe enough to drop guard in. If the answer is no, the brain allocates resources to protection rather than growth, which shows up as defensiveness, avoidance, perfectionism, or shutdown.

People are not resisting change. They are protecting themselves from perceived threat. The difference matters enormously.

In real life
  • An employee becomes defensive during feedback because their brain has predicted that feedback means danger, not because they are difficult.
  • A teenager goes quiet during family conversations because the conversation has been unsafe before, not because they have nothing to say.
  • A high performer underperforms in a new role because the unfamiliar environment is reading as threat, not because they have lost capability.

A team member who normally contributes well in meetings has gone quiet over the last three weeks. They give one-word answers when asked directly, and they have started turning their camera off. Their work is still being delivered on time.

What the pattern is signalling

Option b is the most useful frame. The behavioural signals here, withdrawal, reduced visibility, minimal exposure, are nervous system protection patterns, not motivation patterns. Disengagement tends to show as reduced output. This shows as reduced exposure with output preserved, which is the signature of a system that has decided it is safer not to be seen.

The intervention is not a performance conversation. It is a safety conversation, and the question is what changed three weeks ago.

The mechanism
03

Redesign the Cue

Most daily behaviour runs on a cue, routine, reward loop, and the loop lives in the environment more than in the person. This is why people who move house or change jobs often find old habits dissolve and new ones form almost by accident, and why willpower is the least durable lever for change.

The useful question is not how to become more disciplined, it is what is currently cueing the behaviour, and what would cue something else.

In real life
  • The evening glass of wine is rarely about the wine, it is about the cue, often the moment of sitting down after the day ends.
  • The phone-in-bed habit is rarely about the phone, it is about the cue, often the absence of a different transition into sleep.
  • The procrastination on a specific task is rarely about laziness, it is about a cue the brain has linked to discomfort, which it is now rerouting away from.

What to expect

The new behaviour will not feel natural for the first two to four weeks. That is not failure, that is the pathway forming. The lever is repetition in the same cue, not motivation in the moment.

The fuel
04

Make Progress Visible

The brain needs to see that effort is producing change, otherwise motivation decays even when the behaviour is objectively working. Dopamine is involved in pursuit and anticipation, not just pleasure, and it responds to visible progress more than to outcome.

This is why people abandon habits that are actually succeeding, because the progress is invisible to them, and why tracking, reflection, and small visible markers change behaviour more reliably than goal-setting does.

In real life
  • A client who is genuinely improving will still feel stuck if they cannot see the improvement, and will often quit just before the change consolidates.
  • A team that is making real progress on a long project will lose momentum without visible milestones, even if the work is on track.
  • A personal change effort that has no daily evidence will be abandoned long before it would have failed on its own merits.

Why this matters

The ritual is not the tracking, it is the reviewing. The brain needs to register the evidence to keep investing energy in the new pathway. Without the review, the tracking is just data.

Your synthesis

Your stack

Read from the bottom up when something is not working.

Most stalls happen at the lower layers, not the top. If the habit is failing, check whether the cue has shifted. If the cue redesign is not holding, check whether the environment is signalling safety. If safety feels fine and nothing is moving, check your state.

You can return to this stack anytime, and revise any layer. The work is not linear, even when the sequence is.

A reminder, as noted at the start. This is a coaching framework, not a precise account of the brain. The directions are well-supported, the mechanisms are simplified. Use it as a lens.
